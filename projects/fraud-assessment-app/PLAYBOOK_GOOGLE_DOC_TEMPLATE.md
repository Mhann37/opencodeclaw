## INTRODUCTION: Why Wire Fraud is Your #1 Risk

### The Numbers Don't Lie

Real estate professionals handle some of the largest financial transactions most people ever see. The average residential closing involves wires of $200,000–$500,000. Commercial transactions? Millions.

But here's the painful truth: $1.6 billion+ is stolen annually through real estate wire fraud in the United States alone. And the worst part? Most of it is preventable.

Unlike burglary or embezzlement, wire fraud doesn't require criminal genius. It requires:
1. A spoofed email
2. Five minutes of your time
3. One click

That's it.

### How This Happens (In Real Time)

Here's a real scenario that plays out 50+ times per day across the country:

Tuesday, 2:47 PM — An agent receives an email that looks like it's from their title company. The email address? closing@titlecompany-services.com (the real one is closing@titlecompany.com — notice the -services part).

The message is urgent: "Wire funds immediately to expedite closing. New banking details due to system migration."

The agent, busy with three other closings, glances at the email. It looks legitimate. The sender email is almost right. The tone matches what they've seen before. There's no obvious red flag.

They wire $425,000.

3 hours later, the actual title company calls to confirm they received the funds. That's when the agent realizes: they just sent a quarter-million dollars to a criminal.

### The Recovery Reality

Once that wire leaves your bank account, recovery is nearly impossible. Most banks have a 24–48 hour window to attempt a reversal. By then, the money has been transferred to another account, moved to another bank, and disappeared internationally.

Of the $1.6 billion stolen annually, less than 5% is ever recovered.

The average loss per incident? $145,000.

Some agencies have been wiped out completely by a single wire fraud. One Chicago firm lost $2.3 million in a coordinated attack. They're still in business, but barely.

## THE 3 BIGGEST FRAUD RISKS IN REAL ESTATE

### A. Wire Fraud / Payment Diversion (The #1 Threat)

What It Is:
An attacker intercepts or spoofs email communication to redirect wire transfers to their own bank account instead of the legitimate recipient (title company, lender, seller, etc.).

How It Happens:

There are several common approaches:

Method 1: Email Account Compromise
The attacker gains access to someone's email account (title company staff member, agent, broker). Once inside, they set up an email forwarding rule. Every wire instruction email gets forwarded to the attacker, who reads it and sends a modified version with new banking details before the legitimate sender can send the real instructions.

Method 2: Domain Spoofing / Close Lookalike
The attacker registers a domain that looks almost identical to the legitimate one:
- Real: closing@titlecompany.com
- Fake: closing@titlecompany-services.com (adds -services)
- Real: escrow@smithlandtitle.com
- Fake: escrow@smith-landtitle.com (replaces space with dash)

The attacker sends an email with new banking instructions from this fake address. Many people skim emails and don't notice the subtle difference.

Method 3: Man-in-the-Middle / Intercept
The attacker gains access to email servers or networks and intercepts legitimate wire instruction emails in transit, then quickly sends a replacement with new banking details.

Method 4: Social Engineering
The attacker calls an agent, poses as a title company representative, and provides wire instructions with fraudulent banking details. They often reference details from the transaction that make them sound legitimate.

### Real Example: The $425,000 Disappearing Act

What Happened:
A residential agent in Texas was handling a $1.2M home sale. Three days before closing, she received an email from what she thought was the title company:

"Due to system updates, we need all wires directed to a temporary account. New details attached."

The email address was almost correct. The tone matched previous communications. There was urgency (system updates), which seemed believable.

The agent instructed her buyer's lender to wire the down payment: $425,000.

The Red Flags (In Hindsight):
1. Email address had a subtle variation (one extra letter)
2. Request came outside normal timing (mid-afternoon instead of morning)
3. No prior notification about system updates
4. New banking information (not typical for mid-transaction)
5. Vague urgency ("system updates" without context)

What Happened Next:
Three hours later, the title company called to ask why they hadn't received the down payment. The agent's stomach dropped.

She immediately contacted her bank's fraud department, but the money had already been transferred. The receiving bank, also compromised, allowed the funds to be moved internationally within 20 minutes.

The Outcome:
- Police report filed: Classified as federal wire fraud
- FBI IC3 report submitted
- Bank's fraud department: "Nothing we can do; window has passed"
- Insurance claim filed: Covered 60% after a 6-month investigation
- Agent's reputation: Damage control for months ("Why didn't you verify?")

How It Could Have Been Prevented:
One phone call. The agent could have called the title company's main number (from her existing records, not the email), asked for the wire contact by name, and asked them to confirm the banking details. That 60-second call would have saved $425,000.

### Red Flags to Watch For (Wire Fraud)

Email Red Flags:
⚠️ Sender's email address is almost but not quite right (extra dash, letter swap, domain variation)
⚠️ Generic greeting ("Dear Agent" or "Hello" instead of your name)
⚠️ Spelling or grammar errors (professional title companies proofread)
⚠️ Unusual urgency ("Must wire today," "Time-sensitive," "Don't delay")
⚠️ Request to use an unusual payment method ("Wire to this personal account," "Use Zelle," "Buy gift cards")
⚠️ Links or attachments you weren't expecting
⚠️ Email received outside normal business hours or right before a holiday
⚠️ Sender requesting you don't contact anyone else about the wire ("Keep this between us")

Transaction Red Flags:
⚠️ Banking details change from what you have on file
⚠️ Wire amount doesn't match closing docs
⚠️ Recipient name is different from expected
⚠️ Request comes from someone you don't normally work with
⚠️ Request comes at an unusual time in the transaction (e.g., "emergency wire" mid-closing)
⚠️ Multiple people asking about the same wire (could be group fraud attempt)
⚠️ Pressure to bypass your normal approval process

Communication Red Flags:
⚠️ Tone is different from previous communications with this person
⚠️ Sender is unusually pushy or dismissive of your verification questions
⚠️ They discourage you from calling to confirm (biggest red flag)
⚠️ Message says "Don't call, just wire" or "Don't tell anyone about this"

### B. Invoice Fraud / Vendor Impersonation

What It Is:
A fake invoice is sent to your agency (or title company) requesting payment for services, supplies, or fees that either don't exist or are impersonations of legitimate vendors.

How It Happens:

The attacker researches vendors your agency typically uses (title company, escrow, inspection company, survey company, lender) and creates a near-identical invoice with slightly different banking details.

Common scenarios:
- Vendor Account Takeover: Attacker gains access to a real vendor's email and sends a fake invoice with new payment instructions ("Account migration," "New banking details")
- Invoice Intercept: Attacker intercepts a real invoice in email and sends a replacement with fraudulent banking details before the legitimate one arrives
- Impersonation: Attacker creates a fake invoice from a vendor your company occasionally uses
- Timing Attack: Invoice arrives right before closing or a deadline, pressuring quick payment without verification

### Real Example: The $12,000 Title Company Fee

What Happened:
An office manager at a small real estate firm received an invoice from "Smith Land Title" (a company they use regularly) requesting $12,000 for "account management fees and service charges" for recent closings.

The invoice looked legitimate:
- Proper letterhead
- Real invoice number format
- Familiar vendor name
- Banking details included

The office manager processed the payment without hesitation.

The Red Flags (In Hindsight):
1. No prior communication about these fees
2. Amount was vague ("account management") with no itemization
3. Invoice came via email (not their usual secure portal)
4. Banking details were different from previous payments
5. Email address was almost correct (one-letter variation)

What Happened Next:
Two weeks later, the real Smith Land Title contacted the agency asking about payment for the same closings. That's when they realized they'd sent $12,000 to a criminal.

The Outcome:
- Bank initially said they couldn't recover the funds (wire already processed)
- A formal complaint to the FBI took 6 months
- Zero recovery

How It Could Have Been Prevented:
1. One phone call to Smith Land Title using a known phone number: "I received an invoice for $12,000 for account fees. Can you confirm this is legitimate?"
2. One login to the title company's online portal (instead of using the invoice from the email) to verify the fee

---

### Red Flags to Watch For (Invoice Fraud)

⚠️ Invoice from a vendor you don't normally use or use infrequently
⚠️ New vendor requesting payment via wire (most vendors prefer ACH or credit card)
⚠️ Invoice arrives right before a deadline or closing (creates pressure)
⚠️ Banking details don't match what's in your vendor file
⚠️ Invoice is for a new or unusual type of service
⚠️ Request to change payment method (e.g., "Please wire instead of ACH")
⚠️ No itemization or detail (just a lump sum)
⚠️ Invoice received via email instead of through a secure portal
⚠️ Slight variations in vendor name or email address

### C. Account Takeover / Credential Theft

What It Is:
An attacker gains access to an employee's email or system account and impersonates them to send fraudulent wire instructions or requests.

How It Happens:

Method 1: Phishing
An employee receives an email that looks like it's from Google, Microsoft, or their bank, asking them to "verify" their password. The link goes to a fake site that captures their credentials. The attacker now has their password and can log in.

Method 2: Weak Password / Password Reuse
An employee uses the same password for multiple accounts. Their password gets compromised in a data breach at an unrelated company. The attacker tries the same password on their work email and gains access.

Method 3: Social Engineering
An attacker calls the employee, posing as IT support, and asks them to "verify" their password over the phone or by responding to an email. The employee, thinking it's legitimate, provides it.

Method 4: Malware
The employee visits a compromised website or opens an infected attachment. Malware installs a keylogger that captures their password.

Method 5: Unprotected Device
Employee laptop gets lost, stolen, or left unlocked in a coffee shop. Attacker gains access to email and all connected accounts.

### Real Example: The Email That Wasn't from the Boss

What Happened:
At a real estate brokerage in California, the office manager received an email from the broker (her boss):

"I need you to send a wire ASAP. Buyer's lender is requesting an emergency wire to expedite closing. Send $180,000 to [banking details]. This is urgent."

The office manager knew the broker often needed quick wires, so she processed it without question.

What Actually Happened:
The broker's email account had been compromised via a phishing attack three days earlier. The attacker had set up an email forwarding rule to read incoming emails but waited to use the account until a real wire was in motion.

When the office manager sent the fraudulent wire, the attacker received a copy of the confirmation, deleted the evidence from the broker's inbox, and disappeared.

The Red Flags (In Hindsight):
1. Email came from the boss's account, but contained no context (unusual for his style)
2. Wire amount was for a new transaction (not mentioned in office meetings)
3. Urgency was extreme ("ASAP," "emergency")
4. No follow-up confirmation email from the boss (his usual pattern)
5. Banking details were different from the lender's normal account

The Outcome:
- Broker initially blamed the office manager
- Office manager noticed the anomaly in her email review
- Discovered the forwarding rule in the boss's email settings
- Police report, FBI report, bank investigation
- 90% recovered after 3 months (quick action helped)

How It Could Have Been Prevented:
1. Office manager could have called the broker directly: "I received an email about a wire. Can you confirm?"
2. Broker should have had multi-factor authentication (MFA) enabled on his email account
3. Broker should have reviewed his email forwarding rules regularly

---

### Red Flags to Watch For (Account Takeover)

⚠️ Email from someone you know, but the tone is different or unusual
⚠️ Request is unusual for that person (executive requesting small wire vs. normal pattern)
⚠️ Email is missing context or details that person usually includes
⚠️ Request comes outside that person's normal working hours
⚠️ Unusual urgency or pressure ("Don't mention this," "Act fast")
⚠️ Request to use an unusual payment method
⚠️ No response to follow-up emails or calls (person might not know their account is compromised)
⚠️ IT department reports unusual login activity from that person's account

## THE 5-STEP WIRE VERIFICATION PROTOCOL

This is the core of fraud prevention. If you implement nothing else from this playbook, implement this.

Principle: Never take wire instructions from an email. Always independently verify.

### Step 1: Confirm the Request Came From a Legitimate Source

What to do:
1. DO NOT call the phone number in the email
2. DO NOT use the email address in the email
3. Instead, use a phone number you already have on file or that's publicly listed

Example Script:
"Hi [Name], I received an email about wiring [AMOUNT] to [RECIPIENT]. Can you confirm you sent that email and that the banking details are correct?"

What to listen for:
- Confirmation that they sent the email
- Confirmation of the banking details
- If they sound confused: STOP. Do not proceed. This is a red flag.

Document:
- Who you called
- When you called
- What they confirmed
- Write down their name

### Step 2: Examine the Email Carefully

What to check:

1. Sender's email address
   - Does it match exactly what you have on file?
   - Check character by character (is it .com or .co? Real company or lookalike?)
   - Compare to previous emails from this company

2. Tone and language
   - Is it consistent with this person's usual communication?
   - Are there spelling or grammar errors?
   - Is the urgency level unusual?

3. Content details
   - Does it reference specific transaction details (closing address, buyer/seller names)?
   - Generic greetings ("Dear Agent") vs. personalized ("Dear [Your Name]")?

4. Links and attachments
   - Are there any links? (Be suspicious; don't click)
   - Are there any attachments you weren't expecting?

### Step 3: Independently Verify Banking Information

This is critical. Never use banking details from an email.

What to do:
1. Call the title company/lender/recipient using a phone number you have on file or that's publicly listed
2. Ask them to confirm the wire details: account number, routing number, recipient name, amount
3. Have them read the details back to you
4. Write down what they confirm

Example Script:
"Hi [Name at title company], I'm preparing to send a wire for closing on [Property Address]. Can you please confirm the wire instructions? I need the account name, account number, and routing number."

What to listen for:
- Specific, detailed information
- Any hesitation or confusion
- Do the details match the email?

Red flag if:
- They say "I'm not sure" or "Let me check"
- The details don't match the email
- They sound confused about which wire you're referring to

### Step 4: Review All Transaction Details

Before authorizing, confirm:
- Amount: Matches the closing settlement statement
- Recipient name: Matches the payee in the closing documents
- Account number: Matches what the title company confirmed
- Routing number: Matches what the title company confirmed
- Timing: Is this the normal time in the transaction for this wire?

Red flags:
- Amount is different from closing docs
- Recipient name is slightly different
- Any detail doesn't match what's in your file
- Timing is unusual

### Step 5: Get Approval From Proper Authority

Before any wire is sent:
1. Provide details to your broker/manager/responsible party
2. Get written approval (email is fine, but document it)
3. Document who approved, when, and what they approved
4. Take a screenshot of the approval

Example:
"Broker approval for wire: [Amount] to [Recipient] for closing on [Property]. Approved by [Name] at [Time]. Details verified via phone with [Title Company] on [Date/Time]."

## DECISION TREE: Should We Send This Wire?

Use this flowchart when you're unsure:

RECEIVED WIRE REQUEST?
├─ Do I recognize the sender?
│  ├─ NO → STOP. Investigate before proceeding.
│  └─ YES → Continue
│
├─ Did I independently verify the request with the sender?
│  ├─ NO → STOP. Call them using a known phone number.
│  └─ YES (verified they really sent it) → Continue
│
├─ Do the banking details match what I have on file?
│  ├─ NO → STOP. Call the title company/lender independently.
│  ├─ CHANGED → STOP. Ask why. Get written explanation. Verify with title company.
│  └─ YES (match on file) → Continue
│
├─ Does the amount match my closing docs?
│  ├─ NO → STOP. Investigate the discrepancy.
│  └─ YES → Continue
│
├─ Is the timing normal for this transaction?
│  ├─ NO (emergency wire mid-closing) → STOP. Verify urgency.
│  └─ YES → Continue
│
├─ Do I have written approval from broker/manager?
│  ├─ NO → STOP. Get approval.
│  └─ YES → Proceed with wire
│
└─ WIRE APPROVED ✅

If you answered NO or STOP to any question, do not proceed until you have resolved it.

## INCIDENT RESPONSE: What to Do If Fraud Happens

You think fraud just happened. Your stomach drops. Here's what to do:

### First 24 Hours (CRITICAL WINDOW)

Minute 1-5: Take a breath, then act
1. Stop. Do not send any more wires.
2. Gather anyone involved (agent, office manager, broker)
3. Confirm what happened: "Who discovered this? What did they discover? When?"

Minute 5-15: Call your bank immediately
1. Call your bank's fraud hotline (not the main number; find the fraud department)
2. Say: "We have a suspected wire fraud. A wire of [AMOUNT] was sent to [ACCOUNT] at [BANK] on [TIME/DATE]. We believe this was fraudulent."
3. Get the name of the person you speak with
4. Ask: "What's the window for reversal? Can you freeze any accounts?"
5. Document everything they say

Minute 15-30: Notify the recipient's bank
1. Call the receiving bank (if you can identify it)
2. Provide the same information
3. Ask them to freeze the account if possible
4. Get confirmation and names

Minute 30-60: Notify the title company/lender
1. Call the actual title company/lender to notify them
2. They may have contacts at the receiving bank that can help

First 2 hours: Call law enforcement
1. Call your local FBI field office (Google "[Your City] FBI wire fraud")
2. Report it as wire fraud
3. They may be able to coordinate with federal agencies faster
4. Also file a report at IC3.gov (Internet Crime Complaint Center)

First 4 hours: Document everything
1. Get copies of all emails related to the fraud
2. Take screenshots of banking details you sent
3. Get names and times of all phone calls made
4. Document exactly when the fraud was discovered

First 24 hours: Notify your broker/owner
1. Brief them on exactly what happened
2. Show them your timeline
3. Explain recovery efforts in progress

### Within 48 Hours

1. Gather all communications
   - Every email (even deleted ones; your IT can recover)
   - Every text message
   - Every call log

2. Interview everyone involved
   - Who received the initial wire request?
   - Who authorized it?
   - When did each person act?
   - What was said in phone calls?

3. Secure your systems
   - Check all email accounts for forwarding rules
   - Force password resets for affected employees
   - Enable MFA if not already enabled
   - Check for malware
   - Review access logs

4. File formal complaints
   - FBI IC3 (ic3.gov)
   - Local police
   - Your state's attorney general (fraud division)

5. Contact your insurance company
   - Most commercial general liability policies have a cyber/fraud rider
   - Time is critical for claims

### Days 3-7: Recovery Efforts

Work with your bank:
- They have a limited window (usually 24-48 hours) for reversal attempts
- Some banks can freeze receiving accounts if contacted early enough
- International wires are nearly impossible to recover after 24 hours

Monitor the receiving account:
- Your bank may be able to identify which bank received the funds
- That bank can be contacted to hold the account pending investigation
- This is rare, but happens in 5-10% of cases where action is taken quickly

Coordinate with law enforcement:
- FBI may be able to work with international authorities
- Recovery rate with law enforcement coordination: 10-15%
- Without: < 1%

### What You Can Actually Recover

Realistically:
- Same-day discovery + immediate action: 5-20% recovery
- Next-day discovery: 1-5% recovery
- More than 24 hours: < 1% recovery

Why so low?
- Funds move internationally within hours
- Criminal networks have relationships with banks that don't ask questions
- Money gets converted to cryptocurrency and disappears

### Prevent Future Incidents

After fraud happens, implement immediately:

1. Strengthen verification protocols (make them even stricter)
2. Increase training (everyone needs to know about this one fraud attempt)
3. Upgrade email security (DMARC, SPF, DKIM to prevent spoofing)
4. Implement MFA (multi-factor authentication for all email accounts)
5. Regular audits (check email forwarding rules weekly)
6. Secondary approval (require a second person to verify wire authorization)

### WIRE VERIFICATION CALL SCRIPT

Use this script when calling to verify a wire request:

CALLING THE SENDER (to confirm they sent the request):

"Hi [Name], I received an email about wiring [AMOUNT] to [RECIPIENT] for the closing on [ADDRESS]. Can you confirm you sent that email and that the banking details are correct?"

Listen for confirmation. If they sound confused, stop.

"Great. Can you read back the account number and routing number so I can verify?"

Write down what they say. Compare to email.

"Thank you. I'll confirm these details with the title company and get back to you."

CALLING THE TITLE COMPANY (to verify banking details independently):

Write down exactly what they say.

"And can you confirm the wire amount should be [AMOUNT]?"

Get confirmation.

"Thank you. I have all the details I need."

AFTER BOTH CALLS:
Compare what you heard from the sender to what the title company confirmed. They should match exactly. If they don't, do not wire. Investigate the discrepancy.

## REAL CASE STUDIES

### CASE STUDY 1: The Spoofed Email That Didn't Work

What Happened:
A residential agent in Austin, Texas received an email that appeared to be from the title company where she'd worked with for 15 years. The email said:

"New banking details due to system migration. For closing on [address], please wire [amount] to [new account details]."

Why She Almost Fell For It:
- Email address was closing@titlecompany-services.com (the real one is closing@titlecompany.com)
- The one-word difference (services) was easy to miss on a quick glance
- She'd received multiple emails from this title company that week
- The tone matched previous communications
- She was busy with three other closings

What Saved Her:
She noticed something: the title company had NEVER changed banking details mid-transaction before. It seemed odd to her, so she called the title company's main number (which she had from closing documents).

"Hi, I received an email about new banking details for the closing on [address]. Is that legit?"

Silence. Then: "What email?"

The Reality:
The title company had not sent the email. Their domain had been spoofed. The attacker had researched the company online and created a near-identical email address.

How Much She Almost Lost:
$320,000

Lesson:
When something seems slightly off, it probably is. One phone call saved a quarter-million dollars.

---

### CASE STUDY 2: The Vendor Impersonation That Worked (Until It Didn't)

What Happened:
An office manager at a real estate firm in Chicago received an invoice from "First Title Services" (a company they'd used once, six months prior) requesting $8,500 for "account setup and annual service fees."

The invoice looked legitimate:
- Professional letterhead
- Real invoice format
- Plausible fee structure

She wired the money without verifying.

How It Happened:
The attacker had researched the agency, identified vendors they worked with, and created invoices from those vendors. They sent the invoices to multiple agencies in the same area.

Out of 50 agencies targeted, only 8 paid. But 8 × $8,500 = $68,000 stolen.

When She Realized:
Two weeks later, First Title Services sent a bill for the same transaction that the fake invoice had covered. That's when the office manager realized she'd been scammed.

What She Should Have Done:
1. Called First Title Services using their main phone number
2. Asked: "Did you send an invoice for $8,500?"
3. If they said no → do not pay
4. If they said yes → ask them to confirm the banking details

Lesson:
Invoices are easy to fake. Always call to verify, especially for amounts over $5,000 or from vendors you don't work with regularly.

### CASE STUDY 3: The Account Takeover That Almost Worked

What Happened:
A broker's email account was compromised via a phishing email that looked like it was from their bank. The broker entered his password into a fake login page without realizing it was fraudulent.

The attacker then:
1. Set up an email forwarding rule to read all incoming emails
2. Waited for a wire authorization in progress
3. When one arrived, sent a fake email to the office manager with modified banking details
4. The office manager wired $180,000

Why He Didn't Know:
- The forwarding rule was hidden in the account settings
- The attacker deleted all evidence from the inbox
- The office manager thought the email came from the broker

When It Was Discovered:
The office manager noticed the broker didn't follow up on the wire confirmation (which was unusual for him). She called him: "Did you ask me to wire $180,000 this morning?"

"What? No. Check my email account."

They discovered the forwarding rule and the deleted emails.

Recovery:
Because they acted quickly (within 2 hours), the bank was able to contact the receiving bank and freeze the account. They recovered 90% of the funds.

Lesson:
Compromised email accounts are devastating. Prevent this with:
1. Strong passwords (or password managers)
2. Multi-factor authentication (MFA) on all email accounts
3. Regular audits of forwarding rules and account settings
4. Phone verification for unusual requests (always call the person)

## APPENDIX: Resources & Tools

### Law Enforcement & Reporting

FBI Internet Crime Complaint Center (IC3)
- Website: ic3.gov
- What to report: Wire fraud, phishing, BEC (Business Email Compromise)
- Why: Creates federal record; FBI investigates patterns; may coordinate international recovery
- Time to report: As soon as fraud is suspected

Your Local FBI Field Office
- Find it: fbi.gov → select your state
- Call the wire fraud division
- Provide: All details, timeline, proof of communications

Local Police Department
- Non-emergency line (not 911)
- File a report; get report number
- Helpful for insurance claims

Your State Attorney General (Fraud Division)
- Website: [State] Attorney General + "fraud"
- Report consumer fraud and wire fraud
- Tracks patterns by state

Internet Crime Complaint Center (IC3) - International
- Many countries have equivalents
- IC3 coordinates internationally on larger fraud cases

### Banking & Recovery

Your Bank's Fraud Department
- Get the direct number and save it
- They handle wire reversals (limited to 24-48 hour window)
- They can coordinate with receiving banks

Receiving Bank (if identified)
- Can freeze accounts pending investigation
- Can assist with recovery if contacted quickly

FBI's Financial Crimes Division
- Handles large-scale wire fraud
- Can coordinate international recovery
- Success rate: 5-15% if contacted within 24 hours

### Security Tools & Services

Password Management
- 1Password (business plans available)
- LastPass
- Prevents password reuse; generates strong passwords

Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA)
- Google Authenticator (free)
- Authy (free)
- Microsoft Authenticator (free)
- Requires second factor (phone code) to log in; prevents account takeover even if password is compromised

Email Security
- DMARC (Domain Message Authentication Reporting Conformance)
- SPF (Sender Policy Framework)
- DKIM (DomainKeys Identified Mail)
- These prevent email spoofing by verifying sender identity

Domain Monitoring
- SecurityTrails
- DomainTools
- Alerts you if someone registers a domain similar to yours

Breach Monitoring
- Have I Been Pwned (haveibeenpwned.com) — check if your email has been in a data breach
- Set up alerts for your agency email address
